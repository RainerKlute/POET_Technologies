# POET Technologies – 1.6T Hybrid Transmitter PIC (PIC Magazine interview transcript)

2026-01-16

- Video: <https://picmagazine.net/video/779/POET%E2%80%99s_New_1.6T_Hybrid_Transmitter_PIC>

**Sarab Chopra, PIC Magazine:** Hello, Raju. Thank you for joining us. For anyone new to this, can you tell us a bit about POET Technologies and what are the recent developments?

**Raju Kankipati, POET Technologies:** Thank you for having me here today. POET Technologies is a photonics company. We are focused on solving one of the biggest challenges in modern computing and communications, which is moving massive amounts of data faster and more efficiently at a lower cost.

Our core innovation is the POET Optical Interposer. It is a patented platform that integrates optical and electronic components at a wafer level using semiconductor manufacturing techniques. This allows us to semiconductorize photonics, bringing the same scalability, reliability, and cost advantages that transformed the electronic industry to optical connectivity.

What that means in practical terms is smaller, more power-efficient and cost-efficient optical engines and modules that are critical for AI infrastructure, for example hyperscale data centers and next generation telecom networks. As AI and cloud computing continue to scale, optical interconnects become a bottleneck, and POET is uniquely positioned to address that particular challenge the industry is facing.

**Sarab Chopra:** That’s commendable. That just brings me to my first question. POET has recently launched 1.6T hybrid transmitters. Why is it so important and what exactly is it?

**Raju Kankipati:** We call it the POET Teralight. It is our 1.6T line of optical engines. These are highly integrated optical engines that benefit from hybrid integration. We call it hybrid integration because we bring best-of-breed technologies together and integrate them seamlessly on our platform.

For example, the 1.6T transmitter uses EML lasers, which are well established in the industry for their superior performance and reliability. In a conventional approach, the EML lasers are assembled one at a time and require several active alignments to bring the light from the laser to the fiber. The POET solution enables wafer-scale assembly of EMLs on our silicon platform without any active alignments.

Similarly, the receivers integrate photodiodes and transimpedance amplifiers on the platform and make it easy to integrate in a module. We essentially bring different technologies and integrate them on our platform. That is why we call it hybrid.

We have solutions for 500-meter reach, which is called DR8, and also the 2-kilometer reach, which is 2xFR4. One of the huge benefits of this POET Teralight family is that the same engines can be used for two different module types. For example, the 1.6T DR8 and 2xFR4.

Customers can design one module and, using our engines, they can get two different flavors, which is a big deal because it saves a lot of engineering resources and makes manufacturing less capex intensive.

**Sarab Chopra:** That’s great. What were the biggest challenges that POET faced while putting the 1.6T throughput together?

**Raju Kankipati:** The biggest challenge was getting the components themselves. These are cutting-edge components in the industry, so getting access to them was one of our challenges. But then again, recognizing the benefit of our interposer, big companies like Mitsubishi partnered with us. They gave one of the unique lasers to us to integrate on our platform. It was a joint collaboration to bring it to market. That was one big achievement and how we overcame that challenge.

The other part was the testing itself. The industry took time to evolve in terms of testing methodologies and test equipment. We were able to secure all that and finally get all the testing complete to prove that our engines are meeting the industry’s specifications and actually exceeding them. Even at shows like OFC, we have demonstrated that.

**Sarab Chopra:** Talking about the industry, who are your main customers and who does this technology focus on?

**Raju Kankipati:** We have three types of customers. That is how we segment them.

The first type of customers use our engines for their optical modules. We have previously announced companies like Adtran, Luxshare, and Foxconn. These customers use our transmit and receive engines and design optical modules for high-speed datacom and AI markets.

The second type of customers are for our external light source products. These are for CPO applications or chip-to-chip communication, the users of external light sources. We have been working with Celestial AI, which recently got acquired by Marvell. We are also actively engaging with a few other large customers for this product line, and we will announce when the  time is right with those customers.

The third set of customers are the ones we have engaged for custom product development using our interposer and either the customer’s components or some unique components in the industry. For example, NTT is a customer we have announced where we are developing a custom next-generation 100G BiDi chip. This is for the next-generation mobile networks.

Overall, we have almost like a dozen customers that we are actively engaged with, and the POET team is really busy developing and taking these products to production.

**Sarab Chopra:** That’s really insightful. That brings me to my next question: How does your hybrid design actually improve the performance of the technology you are talking about?

**Raju Kankipati:** We talked about bringing the light from the laser all the way to the fiber, right? One of the biggest challenges is the loss of light through this path.

Our interposer, compared to traditional silicon photonics, is a very low-loss platform. Which means you lose less amounts of light as it propagates through the waveguides of our interposer. This helps with power consumption because the laser does not have to burn too much power to compensate for the loss and still come out of the fiber. So now you’re suddenly consuming less power.

The other benefit is that we do not use any wire bonds. In a typical application, a driver is connected to a laser with wire bonds, which are electrical bonds. From the TIA to the photodiode, again, electrical wire bonds are used. We eliminate all of that because we have electrical layers on our platform.

What that does is it improves the performance significantly because of the low inductance between the TIA and photodiode and between the laser and driver. Overall, the performance improves significantly. I think those are two main benefits.

At the module level, implementing our engine and creating a module is probably the simplest part of module design, because all customers have to do is put the engine and attach the fibers, and they have the entire optical system on our chip. It simplifies the overall module design.

**Sarab Chopra:** That’s amazing to hear the technology evolving so quickly. Do you plan to scale this 1.6T to 3.2T, and if yes, how would that be possible?

**Raju Kankipati:** The electrical lane speed the industry is using right now is 200 gigabits per second. To scale to 3.2T, you have to take two approaches.

Either you increase the number of lanes, from 8 lanes of 200 gig to 16 lanes of 200 gig. That is one way of achieving 3.2T, and we can totally achieve that using our existing products because our chips are so small that you can put two transmitters and two receivers in a module and get to 3.2T. So that's doable.

But I don't think that's how the industry wants to evolve to the next speed, right? They want to go to the next bit rate, which is 400 gigabits per second. Our technology becomes even more critical, even more important at that speed because at that speed, you cannot do wire bonds anymore. At those frequencies, performance degrades significantly if you start wire bonding.

We are developing other methods. For example, TSV is one method, where we have through holes on the interposer to bring the data down into a BGA-type of package. We are also developing other technologies which we have demonstrated to have very good performance at those high speeds.

At that speed, our engine will just sit like a BGA package on a module. And then that eliminates wire bonds
throughout the solution. I am hoping that within 2026, we'll be able to demonstrate that 400 gig per lane challenges for integrating.

The other challenge is the component itself, getting the components that work at that high speed. We are working with a couple of partners to develop modulators at that speed. We expect that 2026 will be a good year for us to demonstrate 400 gig per lane, which essentially takes us to 3.2T.

**Sarab Chopra:** I hope it works in favor of POET. With collaboration with other companies and expansion of this technology, what opportunities did POET have when it launched its hybrid transmitter?

**Raju Kankipati:** It took us a few years to develop all the building blocks and finally put together the entire solution. We started engaging customers about two years ago, and this year is our year to productize, meaning to get into production and start shipping volume of these engines.

Customers now see the value of integration and the potential of the POET Optical Interposer platform. Multiple customers are coming to us asking for unique or differentiated solutions for their applications.

As a small company, we cannot take on all projects, so we pick projects that align with our roadmap and use building blocks we have already developed. Our main goal is wafer-scale assembly. We really want to get to a wafer-scale assembly of optical transceivers.

We are developing other technologies to assist with that. For example, whenever there is a laser, you need a lens in front of it to collect the light. You need an isolator to eliminate and back reflections. You need one more lens to couple the light again, and then the fiber attach.

We are developing technologies that eliminate all these active alignments and multiple steps in that process.  We are, I think, getting there probably this year. We will get to a demonstrable solution that eliminates all these components. That will be a big deal for the industry, because then truly we can do a wafer scale assembly of optical transceivers.

**Sarab Chopra:** That’s interesting. What does the future look like, and how do you plan to make this technology available for customers?

**Raju Kankipati:** We will start with pluggable transceivers, which is the main market we are focusing on right now. For the pluggable market with 1.6T and 3.2T, we have a clear roadmap.

The other side is CPO, co-packaged optics, or chip-to-chip communication, where modulation is done on the chip itself and there is an external light source. The external light source market itself will be huge going forward because just the number of lasers that need to power these chips is huge.

We are actually developing something called a hybrid laser. We have done some demonstrations before. We are trying to eliminate the complexity of a DFB laser. Even though DFB lasers are produced in hundreds of millions today, it's still an expensive process. The yield loss is higher and the multiple regrowth at the epitaxy level is much higher.

We are trying to reduce that complexity and get to a simple amplifier-based laser. And we do most of the hard work on the interposer itself. That is one way we are trying to solve the problem for some of our customers who have come to us asking for solutions for these high-power continuous-wave lasers.
